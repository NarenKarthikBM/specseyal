## Cross-opinion read

The single most important thing Stage 2 surfaces is **convergence**: A (correctness), D (testability), and E (sequencing) each independently found the same defect — every git-ext hook, including the two gate-verify hooks FR-009 requires to hard-block, is specified `optional: true`, and the actual hook-dispatch mechanism (identical prose baked into every stock `.claude/skills/speckit-*/SKILL.md`) only *announces* an `optional: true` hook, never auto-invokes it. Three members reached this from three different citation paths (A: graphify-schema-copy framing; D: line-numbered dispatch-prose diff between `optional: true`/`false`; E: the `priority`-is-dead-code grep) without seeing each other's Stage-1 work. That's not three opinions agreeing by chance — it's the same bug found three independent ways, which is about as close to "confirmed" as an anonymized council gets. I re-verified the underlying mechanism claim myself (see Suggestions). This should be the round's highest-priority item, full stop.

**A, D, and E are all excellent and comparably strong.** Each grounds its sharpest claims in direct file reads with line numbers or exact quotes rather than deck-paraphrase, and every specific claim I spot-checked from all three held up letter-for-letter. If I had to differentiate: D's opinion does the most thorough mechanism-tracing (it's the only one that chases the `HookExecutor` reference all the way to "never implemented anywhere," which I independently confirmed via a repo-wide grep — 11 references, zero definitions, in `.py`/`.sh`/`.js`/`.ts`/`.md`). E's opinion contains the single most consequential finding of the round (the R1-seam blast-radius contradiction, below) and is the only one that ties the `optional: true` bug directly to spec.md's own explicit rejection of the resulting behavior, which sharpens it from "technical gap" to "spec non-conformance." A's opinion is the most complete artifact-by-artifact audit (the `GateSHABinding` field-doesn't-exist finding, Finding 4, is airtight and something neither C, D, nor E caught).

**C is the comparative weak point of the four, on one specific axis.** C's installer-complexity finding and its unbundling of R1's two different couplings (D-R3) are both well-grounded and good catches. But C's headline simplification — ship gate-SHA binding as "record-and-disclose only," defer the enforced hard-block — turns out, once checked against `spec.md` directly, to re-propose a pattern the spec already explicitly closed off by name (see Challenge below). C's opinion never cites `spec.md`'s FR-009 clarification text at all, which is exactly the artifact that would have caught this before it was written. C's priority-ordering observation is also a strictly weaker version of E's — C asserts "no priority value is ever assigned" where E greps the actual installer and hook-dispatch code and shows `priority` is written as a static, unread `5` and never consumed anywhere, with the concrete downstream consequence (verify-gate lands after graphify's context-gen at both shared boundaries) pinned down. Where the two overlap, E's is the version worth keeping.
